A student in Taiwan experimenting with software-defined radio technology inadvertently disrupted
rail control signals, halting three high-speed bullet trains for nearly an hour. The incident
triggered an anti-terrorism response before investigators determined the disruption was unintentional.

The event highlights meaningful gaps in radio frequency security for rail control systems. SDR
hardware is inexpensive and widely available, and the incident demonstrates that critical
transportation infrastructure can be disrupted without sophisticated tooling or intent. ICS/OT
security practitioners working in transportation should treat this as a concrete illustration of
RF attack surface exposure in legacy rail signaling systems.
