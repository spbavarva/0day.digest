Analysis of Lotus Wiper, a destructive malware strain deployed against Venezuelan energy firms and utilities, reveals sophisticated living-off-the-land (LotL) techniques and detailed strategies for widespread data deletion across target networks. The malware leverages native operating system tools to evade detection, making it difficult to catch with signature-based defenses.

Energy sector organizations — particularly those in Latin America — should assess their defenses against LotL-based destructive attacks. Behavioral detection capabilities and robust offline backup strategies are the primary mitigations. The targeting of utilities and energy infrastructure aligns with a broader pattern of state or state-adjacent actors conducting disruptive operations against critical infrastructure in the region.
