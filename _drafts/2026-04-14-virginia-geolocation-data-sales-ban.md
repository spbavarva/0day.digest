Virginia has enacted legislation banning the sale of precise geolocation data — defined as data
accurate to within 1,750 feet — preventing data brokers from commercially distributing location
data granular enough to reveal where consumers live, work, worship, or regularly travel. The law
follows similar state-level restrictions as federal privacy legislation remains stalled.

For security practitioners, commercially available location data from brokers has historically
enabled stalking, targeted physical surveillance of individuals, and employee tracking. The
broker market for precise location data represents a persistent OSINT and surveillance capability
gap between well-resourced actors and individuals.

Organizations operating analytics pipelines or products that monetize precise location signals
should assess their regulatory exposure in Virginia, and anticipate similar legislation in
additional states as the momentum continues building.
